Where is the promised land?

I've always been a bit of a skeptic, someone that would step back
and watch others adopt new technology, to see what would happen next. When 'social media' first became a thing, I remember not wanting to actively engage, instead opting to wait for friends to test this new frontier to find issues that would invariably arise.

In 2021, I started investigating AI in software engineering and found various code completion programs and VSCode extensions, however not much was found in terms of productivity. At best, it was a novelty, however often it was a productivity loss.

It is now 2026, and many engineers will have incorporated AI into their daily workflows. I know many who have done exactly that, however I have once again chosen the cautious path. I can't say I'm 'pure', I've used AI for many a task I find to be labourious or not worth my mental capacity, however I've never given up the sharp mental  blade I've honed for over a decade.

Despite the caution, gradual adoption, even going as far as letting AI doing heavy lifting from time to time, I haven't felt productivity gains I was promised, and I think I know why.

### The Productivity Highway

Since the 1930s, urban planners observed something uncomfortable about highway infrastructure. The premise was simple, and understandable at a human level: connect the cities of America with high-speed interstate highways and journeys would be faster and more efficient. It makes intuitive sense. Highways promote faster travel, create direct routing, reducing journey times.

But when academics studied the eventual effects, an unusual outcome emerged. Formalised as the Downs-Thomson paradox in 1962, and confirmed empirically by Duranton and Turner's 2009 study "The Fundamental Law of Road Congestion", the finding was consistent: traffic expands to fill all available road space. A new lane doesn't reduce congestion. It attracts drivers who weren't travelling before, an the road fills back up. These studies even found people moving house and learning to drive, just to take advantage of the newly available infrastructure. This phenomenon is called induced demand; a system, by virtue of existing, creates demand onto itself.

The highways kept getting built anyway, all the way through the 20th and 21st century, not out of ignorance of the studies and the evidence accumulated across decades, but because the people building them weren't the ones sitting in the traffic. Their incentives pointed in a different direction.

For us, the highway is the development lifecycle. Our time savings don't accumulate as you might expect, they're absorbed by the surrounding system. Meetings that couldn't be scheduled find gaps in your calendar. Requests that were being suppressed reactivate. Scope that felt out of reach suddenly feels viable. The lane gets wider. The commute stays the same.
